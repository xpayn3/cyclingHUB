Training Load Explained
Intervals.icu tracks your fitness using three numbers — CTL, ATL, and TSB. Together they tell you how fit you are, how tired you are, and whether you're ready to perform. Here's what each one means.
CTL
Chronic Training Load
aka Fitness

Your 42-day rolling average of daily training stress. Think of it as your long-term fitness bank — the higher it is, the more your body is adapted to hard training. It goes up slowly when you train consistently and drops when you rest or take time off.

0–30Beginner / returning from break
30–60Recreational cyclist, training regularly
60–90Fit amateur, racing or hard sportives
90–120Serious club racer / part-time athlete
120+Elite / pro level — needs full recovery cycles
💡 Aim to build CTL no faster than 5–8 points per week to reduce injury risk.
ATL
Acute Training Load
aka Fatigue

Your 7-day rolling average of daily training stress. It reacts fast — a big week shoots it up, a few easy days brings it down quickly. When ATL is much higher than CTL you're digging a fatigue hole. When it's lower, you're recovering.

ATL < CTLRecovering — feeling fresh, performance may peak
ATL ≈ CTLMaintaining — steady state training
ATL > CTL by 20–30Hard training block — expected but monitor closely
ATL > CTL by 30+Danger zone — high injury & illness risk
💡 ATL drops about 13% per day of rest — a 3-day easy period makes a noticeable difference.
TSB
Training Stress Balance
aka Form  =  CTL − ATL

The difference between your fitness and your fatigue. A positive TSB means you're fresh and ready to perform. A negative TSB means you're carrying fatigue from recent training. Your best races and efforts usually happen when TSB is slightly positive.

Below −30Overreaching — step back immediately
−30 to −10Training load — tired but building fitness
−10 to +5Sweet spot — training while managing fatigue
+5 to +25Peak form — ideal for races & hard efforts
Above +25Detraining — too much rest, fitness declining
💡 Aim for TSB between +5 and +15 on race day. Start your taper 7–14 days out.
TSS
Training Stress Score
per-ride training load

A single number representing the stress of one ride. 100 TSS = riding at your FTP (threshold) for exactly one hour. Every workout gets a TSS; these feed into your ATL and CTL calculations. It accounts for both intensity and duration.

Under 50Easy recovery ride — minimal fatigue
50–100Moderate training ride — recover in a day
100–150Hard session — 1–2 days recovery needed
150+Very hard / long ride — 2–3 days to fully recover
💡 Without a power meter, TSS is estimated from heart rate. A power meter gives more accurate numbers.
RR
Ramp Rate
CTL change per week

How fast your CTL (fitness) is rising or falling each week. Building fitness too quickly leads to injury and burnout. The sweet spot is a gradual, consistent rise that your body can adapt to.

NegativeFitness declining — rest week or off-season
+3 to +5Ideal — safe, sustainable fitness gains
+5 to +8Aggressive — monitor for fatigue & soreness
Above +8Too fast — high injury risk, ease off
💡 New to structured training? Stay at +3–4/week. Experienced athletes can push to +5–6 for short blocks.
Quick Reference
I want to get fitter
Keep ATL > CTL for weeks at a time. Ramp rate +3–5/wk.
I have a race next weekend
Reduce load now. Target TSB +5 to +15 on race day.
I feel exhausted all the time
Check TSB — if below −20, take 3–5 easy days.
I haven't trained in weeks
CTL is low — start easy and ramp slowly. Rushing causes injury.

About CycleIQ

CycleIQ pulls data directly from intervals.icu using their REST API. Your API key is stored only in your browser's localStorage and is never sent anywhere except intervals.icu's own servers. No backend, no accounts — just your data.
